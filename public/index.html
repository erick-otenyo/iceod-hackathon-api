    KALRO Galla Goats Alternative Livelihoods for ASALs

    Abstract

    The livestock sub sector contributes 10% GDP to the Kenyan economy. Likewise, sheep and goats contribute 30% of red meat consumed in the country, while the rest comes from cattle and camels.

    Therefore, sheep and goats play an important role in the livelihoods of a large number of people in the Arid and Semi-Arid Lands (ASAL). Livestock remains the main source of livelihood for communities living in these areas. The population of goats in Kenya is 27,740,153 (2009 census) mainly found in the ASAL.

    However, the productivity of livestock in these areas is low. A value chain analysis conducted indicated that the major constraints to livestock productivity in these areas include feed availability, animal diseases and limited animal breeds.

    KALRO Galla goats are bred for meat and milk and have been used extensively to upgrade the local breeds. KALRO Kiboko is a source of high quality breeding bucks to the ASAL community.

    Local innovation for food security and livelihood

    Abstract

    SACDEP’s local innovation for food security and livelihood improvement project had twenty farmer based brooding sites linked to two Multiplication and Farmer learning Centers (Kilimambogo and Gigindu).

    Ten brooding sites were attached to one Multiplication and Learning centre and ten brooding sites to the other. Farmers who participated were those with basic skills on keeping local chicken, willing to adopt KALRO indigenous chicken and willing to trains at least 15 neighboring farmers once trained. A SACDEP-Trainer trained and visited each brooding site twice a month and the twenty farmers who were trained became the Practical Skills Providers (PSP) Level 1.

    The PSP level 1 crystallized the training practically and they each trained and mentored 15 farmer yielding 300 PSP level 2 farmers. Each PSP level-2 targeted to mentor 10 PSPs level-3 so that in total 300 PSPs level -2 should mentor 3000 PSPs level-3. However up to the time of reporting the 300 PSP level-2 had mentoring at least one PSP level-3 giving a total of 393 PSP level-3.

    The PSP level-3 to aim to extend the KALRO improved Indigenous chicken technology to the entire area. In total the twenty brooding sites are thus serving 713 farmers. SACDEP-Kenya believes this form of extension is sustainable as the farmers themselves become custodians of knowledge and technology

    Irrigated forage production

    Abstract

    The arid and semi-arid lands comprise of over 80% of the country’s land mass. Most of these regions in Kenya are heavily degraded due to various factors including bush encroachment, inappropriate land use, droughts, and increase in unpalatable species among others.

    Various interventions have been tested to reverse this trend. Key among them is reseeding and pasture improvement which is an option to increase the productivity of land to provide adequate feed for livestock. Several species of grass are used in reseeding and these include: Cenchrus ciliaris, Eragrostis superba, Enteropogon macrostachyus and Chloris roxburghiana.

    These are perennial species that are drought tolerant, grazing tolerant, have high nutritive value and are persistent.

    Species Description

    Chloris roxburghiana

    • Common name: Horsetail grass
    • Perennial; occurs at 0-500m above sea level (asl) in open grasslands with sandy clay, deep clay or firm red clay
    • Inflorescence are 6-20cm and yellow-green to purplish
    • Highly palatable and major grass component in dry areas
    • Has the widest ecological adaptation
    • Crude Protein (CP) 16.1 % in early bloom

    Eragrostis superba

    • Common name: Maasai love grass
    • Perennial, occurs in areas below 2100 m asl
    • Soils: sandy-clay and clay
    • Moderately good for grazing – has tough stems
    • CP 11.3% in early bloom

    Cenchrus ciliaris

    • Common name: Common African foxtail
    • Perennial tufted and sometimes stoloniferous; occurs from 0 – 2300 m asl; forms understorey of bush lands
    • Soil: black cotton
    • CP 7.3 - 9.8% in early bloom

    Enteropogon macrostachyus

    • Common name: Bush rye
    • Perennial and occurs from 0 – 1300 m
    • Frequent in bush lands/dense bush
    • Soils: clay or sandy clay
    • Highly palatable (commonly overgrazed)

    Small holder poultry agribusiness development programme: Commercialization of indigenous poultry in Kiambu, Laikipia and Murang’a Counties

    Abstract

    The Commercialization of indigenous poultry project in Mount Kenya region works to increase indigenous chicken production in five target Counties (Kiambu, Laikipia, Murang’a, Nyeri and Kajiado). This report focuses on Kiambu, Laikipia and Muranga Counties. Improved indigenous chicken were promoted as an alternative source of income, improved nutrition and increased household resilience to the impacts of climate change.

    The project aimed at promoting production of; quality day old chicks, improved indigenous chicken breeding stock and feeds by private entrepreneurs. The target targeted to reach 2,500 farmers with the chicken technology. To achieve this staff, lead farmers, farmers and service providers were trained on various aspects of indigenous chicken breeding, management and marketing. There were also exposure and learning tours for lead farmers who were trained on mass hatching and they then bought efficient hatcheries so that they sold eggs and chicks to other farmers. There were also linkage meetings between producers, buyers and service providers to ensure that there were market outlets for the chicken products.

    In total 2700 farmers were mobilized of whom, 2085 farmers had adopted the technology as at the time of reporting. It was noted that the KALRO improved indigenous chicken farming became a key livelihood activity for the small holder farmers in those Counties

    The provided data shows the number of farmers who adopted the KALRO improved indigenous chicken technology in the different groups in Kiambu, Laikipia and Murang’a Counties.

    Effect of Supplementing Rhodes Grass Hay with Cotton Seedcake on Growth Performance of the Small East African Zebu Weaners in South Eastern Kenya

    Abstract

    A study was conducted to investigate the effect of cotton seedcake as a protein supplement on the growth performance of the Small East African Zebu weaner cattle. The study site was KALRO-Kiboko in Makindu District, Makueni County, Kenya. The animals were allocated randomly to feeding treatments in individual pens. A basal diet of Chloris gayana (Rhodes grass) hay and water were provided ad libitum.

    The animals were then subjected to acclimatization for 14 days. During this period estimated amount of basal diet consumed per animal per day was determined. Immediately after this, data collection commenced. Feed intake was determined by recording the amount of hay given in the morning which was slightly above the amount as determined during the acclimatization period and left over weighed the following morning. Consequently the amount consumed was the difference between the amount offered and the amount leftover.

    The leftover were weighed the following morning just before the amount for that day was offered so as to determine the amount consumed per day. The treatments comprised of control (no supplementation), 300 g and 500 g cotton seedcake per animal per day. Mineral lick and water were provided ad libitum. The feeds used were analysed to determine their chemical composition and feed value. It was clear from the results that cotton seedcake was higher in CP (five-fold), EE (four-fold) and ME (two-fold) than the control diet.

    Data on live weight and feed intake were analyzed using General Linear Model of Statistical Analysis System. The results of the data analysis showed increased (P<0.05) feed intake in that the amount of hay consumed increased with the increase in the amount of supplement. Live weight gain also increased (P<0.05) with the increase in the level of supplementation.

    Effects of selected routine husbandry practices on growth rate of small East African goat weaners

    Abstract

    This study was conducted to determine the effects of overnight housing, deworming, supplementation, and their combinations on average weight gain of weaned Small East African Goats (SEAG) in the semi-arid southeastern rangelands of Kenya. The study also evaluated the economic viability of these goat husbandry practices. Forty (40) weaned SEAG of similar age (7±1 months) and weights (21± 3 kg) were randomly assigned to the following eight treatments: housing (Th); deworming (Td); supplementation (Ts); housing + supplementation (Ths); housing + deworming (Thd); supplementation + deworming (Tsd); housing + supplementation + deworming (Thsd); and traditional husbandry (Tc –) which was the control.

    All the animals were weighed every week and weight gains calculated as the difference between the current and previous weight. The experiment lasted for 18 weeks. All treatment groups consistently exhibited higher average weekly weight gains than the control over the entire trial period. However, the enhanced weight gains were not higher (P<0.05) than the control up to the end of third week. On the fourth week, however, all treatment groups except housing, registered higher (P<0.05) weight gains than the control. Overall, a combination of the three treatments (Thsd) had the highest impact on the goats’ weight gain (6.95Kg), followed by Tsd (6.65Kg). The cost-benefit analysis indicated that the most cost-effective treatment was deworming with a benefit-cost ratio (BCR) of 9.45.

    Supplementation and supplementation combined with deworming, with 2.35 and 2.75 BCR, respectively, were also cost-effective. The study concluded that housing; control of internal parasites; enhancement of nutrition; or a combination of these practices, has the potential to improve the weight gain of weaned SEAG which would translate into increased off-take, and, ultimately, income.

    Up scaling the grain amaranth technology in Kitui County

    Abstract

    Catholic Diocese of Kitui is working with 57 groups of people living with HIV/AIDS (PLHIV), two groups of People living with disability (PLWD), 36 farmer groups, two primary and two secondary schools and one polytechnic on up scaling the grain amaranth technology. These groups were sensitized on the nutritional value of the grain amaranth as well as the potential to earn income, and were trained on how to prepare and use amaranth products. Participating farmers have given testimonies of how their health improved as a resulting of consuming grain amaranth products. Others have testified of earning income from amaranth that they used to pay school fees and feed their families. The project was implemented in Kitui, Mwingi, Mutomo and Muthale.
